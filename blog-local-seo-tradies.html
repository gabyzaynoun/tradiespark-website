You're a great tradie. You do quality work, you show up on time, and your customers love you. But none of that matters if people in your area can't find you online.

Local SEO is the process of making your business visible when someone nearby searches for your services. It's the reason one electrician in Geelong gets 40 calls a month from Google while another equally skilled sparky gets two. This guide breaks down exactly how it works and what you need to do — step by step, in plain English.

1. What Is Local SEO (and Why Should You Care)?

When someone in Brisbane searches "plumber near me" or "electrician Paddington," Google uses local SEO signals to decide which businesses to show. These signals include:

  • Relevance: Does your business match what they're searching for?
  • Distance: How close is your business to the searcher?
  • Prominence: How well-known and trusted is your business online?

There are two main areas where local results appear on Google:

  1. The Map Pack — the top 3 business listings with a map that appear above regular search results. This is where most clicks happen.
  2. Organic results — the regular website listings below the Map Pack. Having a website with good local SEO gets you here.

Showing up in both the Map Pack and organic results is the holy grail. It gives you two chances to get clicked, and it signals to searchers that you're a dominant business in the area.

2. Google Business Profile Optimisation

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important factor for ranking in the Map Pack. Here's a step-by-step checklist to optimise yours:

Step 1: Claim and Verify Go to business.google.com and claim your profile. Google will verify you via postcard, phone, or email. This usually takes 3-7 days.
Step 2: Complete Every Section Fill in your business name (exactly as it appears in real life — no keyword stuffing), address, phone number, website, hours, and a detailed business description. Incomplete profiles rank lower.
Step 3: Choose the Right Categories Your primary category is the most important. Choose the one that most closely matches your core service. Then add 3-5 secondary categories. For example, an electrician might use: Primary — "Electrician." Secondary — "Lighting Contractor," "Electrical Installation Service," "Solar Energy Contractor."
Step 4: Add Service Areas List every suburb, city, and region you serve. Be specific. "Melbourne" is too broad. "Fitzroy, Collingwood, Richmond, Carlton, Brunswick" tells Google exactly where you work.
Step 5: Upload Photos Regularly Add at least 10 photos to start: your logo, team photos, completed jobs, your van. Then add new photos every week. Profiles with 100+ photos get 520% more calls and 2,717% more direction requests than those without.
Step 6: Post Weekly Updates Google Business has a "Posts" feature. Use it to share recent jobs, seasonal tips, or special offers. "Just finished a full rewire in a 1920s Queenslander in Paddington. If your home still has old wiring, here's why it matters..." These posts show on your profile and signal to Google that your business is active.

3. Getting More Reviews (the Right Way)

Reviews are the second biggest factor in local SEO rankings. More reviews (and higher ratings) = higher rankings = more calls. It's that simple.

The review strategy that works

  1. Create a direct review link. Go to your Google Business Profile, click "Ask for reviews," and copy the link. Shorten it using a service like bit.ly or set up a redirect on your website (e.g., yoursite.com.au/review).
  2. Ask every happy customer. Immediately after completing a job, while the customer is satisfied, send them a text: "Thanks for choosing us! If you've got 30 seconds, a Google review would really help: [link]"
  3. Respond to every review. Thank positive reviewers by name. For negative reviews, respond calmly and professionally — offer to make it right. Google values businesses that engage with their reviews.
  4. Never buy fake reviews. Google's AI detects fake reviews and will penalise your profile. It's not worth the risk.
Aim for a steady stream of reviews over time rather than a sudden burst. Getting 2-3 reviews per week looks more natural to Google than getting 30 in one day.

4. NAP Consistency

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone Number. It sounds basic, but inconsistent NAP information is one of the most common local SEO killers for tradies.

Your business details must be exactly identical everywhere they appear online:

  • Your website
  • Google Business Profile
  • Yellow Pages
  • True Local
  • Hipages
  • ServiceSeeking
  • Facebook
  • Any other directory or listing

Even small differences matter. If your GBP says "Unit 3, 45 Smith Street" and your website says "3/45 Smith St," Google sees those as potentially different businesses. Pick one format and use it everywhere.

How to audit your NAP

Search your business name on Google and check every listing that appears. Note any inconsistencies and update them. Pay particular attention to old listings where you may have had a different phone number or address. Claim and update (or remove) outdated listings.

5. Local Keywords That Actually Work

Keywords are the search terms people type into Google. For local SEO, you want to target keywords that include both your service and your location.

High-value keyword patterns for tradies

  • [trade] [suburb] — "plumber Bondi," "electrician Parramatta"
  • [service] [suburb] — "blocked drain Marrickville," "hot water system Cronulla"
  • emergency [trade] [area] — "emergency plumber Inner West"
  • [trade] near me — Google uses your location to match this
  • best [trade] [city] — "best electrician Melbourne"

Where to use these keywords on your website:

  • Page titles (the most important on-page SEO signal)
  • H1 headings
  • Meta descriptions
  • First paragraph of page content
  • Image alt text
  • URL slugs (e.g., /plumber-bondi/)

Don't stuff keywords unnaturally. Write for humans first, then make sure your target keyword appears naturally in the key locations listed above. If you need a website that's already built with proper local SEO structure, TradieSpark websites come SEO-optimised out of the box.

6. Service Area Pages

This is one of the most powerful (and underused) local SEO strategies for tradies. Instead of one generic "Areas We Serve" page, create individual pages for each suburb or region you work in.

What a good service area page looks like

  • Title: "Plumber in Penrith — Dave's Plumbing"
  • Content: 300-500 words about your services in that specific area. Mention local landmarks, common plumbing issues in the area (old houses with copper pipes, new estates with builder-grade fittings), and your response time to that suburb.
  • Unique content on each page. Don't just copy-paste the same text and change the suburb name. Google detects duplicate content and it hurts your rankings.
  • Include a call-to-action with your phone number and a quote form.

A plumber with individual pages for Penrith, Blacktown, Liverpool, Campbelltown, and Parramatta will rank in all five suburbs. A plumber with a single page listing all five suburbs will struggle to rank in any of them.

7. Schema Markup for Tradies

Schema markup is code you add to your website that helps Google understand your business better. Think of it as giving Google a cheat sheet about who you are, what you do, and where you do it.

The most important schema type for tradies is LocalBusiness. Here's what it looks like:

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Plumber",
  "name": "Dave's Plumbing",
  "address": {
    "@type": "PostalAddress",
    "streetAddress": "45 Smith Street",
    "addressLocality": "Penrith",
    "addressRegion": "NSW",
    "postalCode": "2750",
    "addressCountry": "AU"
  },
  "telephone": "+61-2-1234-5678",
  "url": "https://davesplumbing.com.au",
  "openingHours": "Mo-Fr 07:00-18:00",
  "areaServed": ["Penrith", "Blacktown", "Mt Druitt"],
  "priceRange": "$$"
}
</script>

You can also add schema for your reviews (AggregateRating), services offered (Service), and individual service area pages. Google uses this information to display rich results — like star ratings and business details directly in search results.

If this looks technical, don't worry. A professionally built tradie website should include proper schema markup from the start.

8. Mobile-First Is Non-Negotiable

Over 72% of local searches happen on mobile devices. When someone has a burst pipe or a power outage, they're not sitting at a desktop computer — they're grabbing their phone.

Google also uses mobile-first indexing, meaning it primarily looks at the mobile version of your site to determine rankings. If your site is slow, clunky, or hard to use on mobile, you'll rank lower — even for people searching on desktop.

Mobile essentials for tradie websites

  • Click-to-call button visible at the top of every page. One tap to call you.
  • Fast loading speed. Under 3 seconds. Compress images, use modern formats (WebP), minimise code.
  • Readable text without zooming. Minimum 16px font size.
  • Easy-to-tap buttons. No tiny links that require precision tapping.
  • Simple navigation. Three to four menu items max.
  • Forms that work on mobile. Large input fields, minimal required fields, no fiddly dropdowns.

Test your site on Google's free PageSpeed Insights tool (pagespeed.web.dev). Aim for a mobile score above 80. If you're below 50, your site is actively hurting your rankings.

Your 30-Day Action Plan

Here's exactly what to do over the next month to dramatically improve your local SEO:

Week 1: Foundation

  • Claim and verify your Google Business Profile (if you haven't already).
  • Complete every section of your GBP: name, address, phone, hours, categories, description, service areas.
  • Upload at least 10 photos of your work, team, and van.

Week 2: Website

  • If you don't have a website, get one built. This is non-negotiable for serious local SEO.
  • If you have a website, check it loads in under 3 seconds on mobile and has your NAP in the footer of every page.
  • Add or update your main service pages with local keywords.

Week 3: Reviews and Listings

  • Create your direct Google review link and save it in your phone.
  • Text 10 past customers asking for a review. Use the script from section 3 above.
  • Audit your NAP across Yellow Pages, True Local, Hipages, and Facebook. Fix any inconsistencies.

Week 4: Content and Ongoing

  • Create your first 2-3 service area pages targeting your highest-value suburbs.
  • Write your first Google Business post.
  • Set a weekly reminder: one new GBP photo + one new GBP post + ask every completed job for a review.

Local SEO isn't a one-time task — it's an ongoing practice. But the tradies who commit to it consistently for 3-6 months see dramatic results: more phone calls, more quote requests, and more jobs. The best part? Unlike paid ads, the results compound over time. The work you do today keeps paying off for months and years to come.
